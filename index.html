Benjamin Franklin's System of Self-Improvement (The 13 Virtues)

Benjamin Franklin did not treat virtue as a personality trait or a moral identity. He treated it as a skill system. Something trainable through structure, tracking, and iteration.

The goal of this discussion is:

  • to understand what problem Franklin was solving
  • to examine how his system worked
  • to evaluate where it succeeds or fails
  • and to consider how similar systems could be applied today

Franklin's Method

Franklin selected 13 virtues he believed captured moral and practical excellence. Rather than attempting to improve all at once, he focused on one virtue per week, cycling through all 13 over 13 weeks.

Key features of his system:

  • Single-variable focus (one virtue at a time)
  • Daily tracking (marking failures, not successes)
  • Iteration, not perfection
  • Lifelong repetition, not short-term challenge

He viewed moral improvement as analogous to learning a craft.

The 13 Virtues

1. Temperance

Eat not to dullness; drink not to elevation.

2. Silence

Speak not but what may benefit others or yourself; avoid trifling conversation.

3. Order

Let all your things have their places; let each part of your business have its time.

4. Resolution

Resolve to perform what you ought; perform without fail what you resolve.

5. Frugality

Make no expense but to do good to others or yourself; waste nothing.

6. Industry

Lose no time; be always employed in something useful.

7. Sincerity

Use no hurtful deceit; think innocently and justly.

8. Justice

Wrong none by doing injuries, or omitting the benefits that are your duty.

9. Moderation

Avoid extremes; forbear resenting injuries so much as you think they deserve.

10. Cleanliness

Tolerate no uncleanliness in body, clothes, or habitation.

11. Tranquility

Be not disturbed at trifles, or accidents common or unavoidable.

12. Chastity

Rarely use venery but for health or offspring; never to dullness or weakness.

13. Humility

Imitate Jesus and Socrates.

Questions for Thought

  • Why does tracking failures work better than celebrating success?
  • Why did Franklin avoid improving multiple virtues at once?
  • What does this system assume about human nature?
  • Where would this system fail today—and why?
  • If you were to design a modern Franklin-style system, what would change?
  • Which virtues would you remove, merge, or add?
  • How would you track progress without obsession?
  • What would make this sustainable for months or years?
