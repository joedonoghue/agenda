The Case for Skill Stacking

The math of percentiles is not linear.

1% is often a lifestyle. 10% is often a project.

Going from 50% → 90% in a skill can happen fast with deliberate practice. Going from 90% → 99% is where the time curve explodes — tiny gains, rare mentorship, elite environments.

The trade-off:

  • Top 1% requires near-total investment with diminishing returns
  • Top 10% in two complementary skills can be achieved with disciplined but realistic effort
  • The "bridge" person who closes loops between domains is often scarce

What AI Changes

AI makes these cheaper:

  • First-draft writing, basic slides
  • Routine coding snippets
  • Straightforward analysis and summaries
  • "Google-able" knowledge work

AI makes these more valuable:

  • Problem selection — choosing what's worth doing
  • Taste and quality bar — knowing what "good" looks like
  • Verification — catching errors, hallucinations, edge cases
  • Context and constraints — legal, security, organizational reality
  • Ownership — being the person accountable for outcomes
  • Trust and relationships — humans still hire humans they trust

High-Value Two-Skill Combinations

Pick one from each column:

Domain Credibility Leverage Skill
Finance / Ops / Logistics Automation (no-code, scripting, APIs)
Healthcare / Med Admin Data sensemaking (SQL, dashboards)
Cyber / Compliance Product thinking (user needs → roadmap)
Defense / Public Sector Communication (writing, presenting)
Education / Training Security / Risk thinking
Marketing / Growth Technical fluency

Examples:

  • Ops + Automation = turn AI into time saved, not "cool demos"
  • Domain expert + Data = ask better questions than generic analysts
  • Sales/BD + Technical fluency = explain, close, and implement AI products

Questions for Thought

  • Which skills are worth a lifestyle? Which are worth a 90-day project?
  • If AI makes the basics cheap, what two skills become an unfair combo?
  • In your own stack, what does AI commoditize — and what does it amplify?
  • What "proof-of-work artifact" would demonstrate your skill combination?
  • How do you avoid being the person who's "pretty good at a lot of things" but owns nothing?

The goal is not to know a little about everything. It's to become the person who can close loops that otherwise require two or three people.
